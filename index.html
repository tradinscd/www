Blog

I am a Master's student in Computational Science at Harvard University. I am interested in methods for parameter estimation and inference, particularly analyzing the tradeoff between their computational efficiency and numerical stability.

I currently work with Professor Edoardo Airoldi, where we are analyzing stochastic gradient methods for principled estimation with large data sets. Prior to this, I was an undergraduate at Berkeley where I studied convex optimization and randomized matrix factorizations.

I am also a teaching fellow for the Numerical Methods graduate course at Harvard, and I do part-time work for startups.

Nothing is more exciting to me than finding a cooler way to solve a problem ("cooler" defined by multiple metrics—speed, simplicity, generality among a few). Hacking is a broad term, so I'll just outline my affection of hacks to programming.

Individual libraries of a language can be awesome, and are primarily why I use a certain language: I gush about dplyr because of its data manipulation grammar which is leaps and bounds ahead of standard tools; D3.js does web visualization like none other; scikit-learn is the best collection of machine learning tools in one library; Boost has insanely awesome metaprogramming for optimal runtime speeds; knitr produces the most beautiful typewritten documents with embedded code and visuals; etc. etc.

Let's not forget about window managers, Vim, and Vim-inspired applications such as ranger, Pentadactyl, zsh in vi-mode, zathura, and the dozens more that can replace almost all applications which would otherwise require a mouse. Productivity is so much higher when you have dozens of macros to teleport among workspaces, applications, and even lines of code.


I enjoy all storytelling mediums, although I'm most fond of books and films. Kafka on the Shore, The Silmarillion, Barry Lyndon, and 8 ½ are some of my favorites.

I usually abide by western convention—solid character development, presentation, plot progression, and overall message. I do however have a preference for atmosphere more than most, and am also not one to fault, say, eastern works from a western penchant. I would say my attitude in general leans toward art house works, in visuals, in prose, or in whatever lends the most strength and is most niche to the medium. I find they really challenge the meaning of art and the expression within it.


I love chess most because of the highly developed metagame. While I find certain board games more conceptually and mathematically interesting (particularly Go), chess is that unique mix of familiarity and high-end complexity that continues to make new developments engaging.

I played competitively for a few years but have recently kept myself within the online community and its ladders. lichess is my preferred platform for games, although I haven't found it to be a suitable replacement for Chess Tempo's tactics. I'm always up for a game if you encounter me in real life!
