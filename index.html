I am a Ph.D. student in Statistics at Harvard, where I work with Edo Airoldi and David Blei. I am broadly interested in machine learning and Bayesian statistics. My focus is on approximate posterior inference as well as (nonparametric) models of relational data such as networks.

I am fortunate to be a member of many incredible groups: the Harvard Lab for Applied Statistical Methodology, the Blei Lab, the Stan development team, and the Harvard Intelligent Probabilistic Systems (HIPS) group. During my undergraduate at Berkeley, I studied topics in random graphs with David Aldous.

Papers

Variational inference with copula augmentation

We develop a general methodology for variational inference which preserves dependency among the latent variables. This is done by augmenting the families of distributions used in mean-field and structured approximation with copulas. Copulas allow one to separately model the dependency given a factorization of the variational distribution, and can guarantee us better approximations to the posterior as measured by KL divergence.
Dustin Tran, David M. Blei, Edoardo M. Airoldi
Neural Information Processing Systems, 2015
Paper Poster

Towards stability and optimality in stochastic gradient descent

Iterative estimation methods, such as stochastic gradient descent (SGD), update the current iterate (parameter estimate) according to some discrepancy between what is observed and what is expected, assuming the current iterate has the true parameter value. Although SGD is computationally efficient, it cannot effectively combine it with statistical efficiency and stability. We propose a new procedure to resolve these two issues.
Panos Toulis, Dustin Tran, Edoardo M. Airoldi
Preprint, 2015

Software

Automatic Variational Inference


Alp Kucukelbir and I are developing automatic variational inference in the Stan probabilistic programming language. It features cool things like automatic differentiation and parameter transformations in order to make Bayesian inference viable on massive data sets. Check it out on Github.

JuliaStats


Julia is really a revolution for scientific computing. It lacks many fundamental tools before it becomes mainstream in the statistics and machine learning community, but we're making it happen! Check it out on Github.

sgd Package in R


We build large-scale estimation tools in R using stochastic gradient descent, available on CRAN. The library I'm developing with Panos Toulis includes a slew of stochastic gradient methods, built-in models, visualization tools, hypothesis testing, convergence diagnostics, and other cool stuff. Check it out on Github.

NIPS Preview


My fork of Andrej Karpathy's nipspreview uses a hierarchical Dirichlet process to topic model conference papers. This is by far the most elegant way to browse lists of accepted papers. There's still a lot of work to be done in terms of navigation and prettifying it, but the algorithm itself is more or less optimized.

MCMC Visualizer


mcmc-viz is a 3D visualization tool for Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. I am currently working with a friend of mine, Ye Kuang, in order to extend our framework to compare for example HMC, Multiple-Try Metropolis, and parallel variants.
